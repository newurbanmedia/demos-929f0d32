01
The Foundation

Your concept is the source.

Every decision in this document begins with the logo you created. The heart. The brushstrokes. The trans flag colors. Those choices already carry meaning — our work was to refine, not replace.

Before any refinement, it was important to honor what was already on the table. The original mark you developed established the symbolic language of the brand: a hand-painted heart in trans pride colors, layered with the energy of community and resilience. Those decisions weren't aesthetic accidents — they were a statement of intent.

What follows is a step-by-step record of how that foundation was preserved and strengthened. Type was reconsidered. Color was re-balanced. Texture was tested. The heart, the colors, the spirit — those stayed.

02
Typography

Choosing a voice that matches the mission.

The first refinement was the wordmark. The original used a classical serif (Didot-style) — elegant, but editorial. The brand voice called for something with more warmth, more presence, more humanity.

The problem

The original serif read as fashion editorial, not community-rooted nonprofit.

Classical high-contrast serifs (Didot, Bodoni, Playfair) carry strong associations with luxury fashion and editorial publishing. Beautiful — but emotionally distant from a grassroots organization built around dignity, protection, and intergenerational care.

The wordmark needed to feel warm but serious, mature but contemporary, readable to elders but fresh to youth.

The solution

Fraunces — a contemporary serif with a soft, human feel.

Fraunces is an open-source variable serif designed for warmth. Its rounded terminals, soft serifs, and confident weight give the wordmark presence without coldness.

It reads as institutional enough for donor communications and grant applications, while remaining warm enough for community-facing materials, social media, and merch.

03
Color exploration

Resolving the split between place and identity.

The original wordmark used three different color treatments — "Mid-South" in black, "Trans" in pink, "Nation" in blue. The result was elegant, but it visually separated the place from the identity. Mid-South Trans Nation is one thing, not three.

Four directions were explored. Each tested a different way of integrating the trans flag colors across the full wordmark — so the place, the identity, and the community would read as a single, unified mark.

Figure 03 Four color directions tested — each integrating the trans pride palette into a unified wordmark.
Direction selected

A vertical gradient that runs through every letter.

The chosen direction (bottom row, Figure 03) runs a smooth gradient from a soft highlight at the top, through pink in the middle, into trans-flag blue at the bottom — applied to every letter equally.

This treatment unifies the wordmark, preserves all three trans flag colors, and gives the type a subtle three-dimensional quality — as if the letters are catching natural light from above.

Why this works

Identity becomes inseparable from name.

When every letter carries the same gradient, no single word becomes "the trans part" — the entire mark is the trans part. Place and identity become one.

The gradient also echoes the visual feeling of sunrise — a recurring metaphor in the brand's mission: a new day for the next generation.

04
Texture · the wordmark

Granulated over solid — personality over generic.

Two versions of the wordmark were developed: a solid version with hard color bands, and a granulated version with a soft, grainy gradient. Both work — but they tell different stories.

Option A · Technical / merch

Solid wordmark

Hard color bands. Reads as the trans flag, literally — three flat horizontal stripes inside the letterforms.

Strengths
  • Instant flag recognition
  • Reproduces cleanly in print, embroidery, vinyl
  • Works at very small sizes
Limitations
  • Feels institutional, not distinctive
  • Hard bands break letter flow
  • Less brand personality
Option B · Primary / brand

Granulated wordmark

A soft, grainy gradient that flows from highlight to pink to blue. Hand-painted in feel — alive with texture.

Strengths
  • Memorable, distinctive, brand-specific
  • Echoes the brushstroke heart
  • Carries warmth and humanity
Limitations
  • Grain may not survive embroidery
  • Needs the solid version as backup
  • Texture softens at small sizes

The recommendation is to use both — strategically. The granulated wordmark serves as the primary brand expression for digital, social, posters, and print. The solid wordmark serves as the technical backup for embroidery, single-color reproduction, very small applications, and any context where texture cannot be preserved.

This is not a compromise. It's how every mature visual identity works: one brand voice, multiple production tools.

05
The heart · awaiting your decision

Two interpretations. Both yours. Pick the one that feels right.

The heart itself was kept exactly as you envisioned it: three brushstrokes, trans flag colors, painted feel. But two subtle variants were developed — and the choice between them is yours.

These two versions sit on the same warm peach-to-lavender gradient background. The difference is in how the brushstrokes themselves are rendered. Read both descriptions carefully — the difference is small but meaningful.

Variant A

Solid heart

Brushstrokes are cleaner, more unified, more confident. Each stripe of color reads as one continuous painted gesture.

What it says
  • Strength and clarity
  • Unity and confidence
  • Polished without being corporate
  • Reads cleanly at any size
Consider
  • Slightly less raw and emotional
  • Less visible "hand of the artist"
  • Smoother, less wounded
Variant B

Fragmented heart

Brushstrokes carry more internal texture — more tears, more breaks, more space where the background bleeds through.

What it says
  • Resilience through struggle
  • Raw, lived experience
  • More hand-painted, more human
  • Mended — not perfect
Consider
  • May read as "broken" to some audiences
  • Harder to reproduce at small sizes
  • Less polished for institutional use

This is a values decision, not a design decision. The solid heart leads with strength and confidence. The fragmented heart leads with honesty about what the community has survived. Neither is wrong. The right answer is the one that matches how you want MSTN to be felt at first glance.

The process · summary

How we got here.

A condensed look at the path from your original concept to the proposal in your hands today.

01
Starting point

The original logo concept

Your hand-developed mark: a brushstroke heart in trans flag colors, with the MSTN acronym in a graffiti-inspired hand on a black background. The symbolic language was already set.

02
First refinement

Background shift · black to warm gradient

The black background was replaced with a peach-to-lavender gradient. This shift introduced warmth, opened the brand to a sunrise metaphor, and made the mark more approachable to elders, families, donors, and youth simultaneously.

03
Typography

From classical serif to Fraunces

The classical high-contrast serif was replaced with Fraunces — a contemporary serif with soft, rounded terminals that reads warm without losing institutional credibility.

04
Color treatment

Unifying the wordmark

The split-color treatment ("Mid-South" black, "Trans" pink, "Nation" blue) was replaced with a single vertical gradient applied to every letter — uniting place and identity into one mark.

05
Texture decision

Granulated wordmark for brand, solid for technical

Two production-ready versions of the wordmark were finalized: a granulated/grainy primary version for brand expression, and a solid version for embroidery, single-color print, and small-scale reproduction.

06
Heart variants

Two interpretations of the same heart

The brushstroke heart was developed in two textures — solid (clean, confident) and fragmented (raw, hand-painted) — awaiting your final selection.

The system ahead

Logo variations to be built after approval.

Once the heart is selected and the wordmark is approved, the logo will be expanded into a full system — designed to perform across every context MSTN operates in.

Below is the full list of variations that will be developed and delivered as part of the brand guide. None of these have been built yet — they will be developed after this proposal is approved, so we're not designing for a direction that might change.

i.
Primary lockup · vertical
Heart above, wordmark below, on the peach-to-lavender background. The flagship version — for posters, social profile images, and brand-led applications.
ii.
Primary lockup · horizontal
Heart on the left, wordmark on the right. For website headers, email signatures, banners, and horizontal real-estate applications.
iii.
Isotype · heart only
The heart alone, used when the brand is already established in context (social profile pictures, favicon, app icon, merch tags).
iv.
Wordmark · standalone
The granulated wordmark alone, for editorial layouts, document headers, and contexts where the heart would crowd the design.
v.
Monochrome · single color
A solid one-color version (black, white, or any single brand color) for embroidery, single-color print, vinyl cutting, fax, and stamps.
vi.
Reverse · for dark backgrounds
A version designed to sit on dark photography, dark website sections, and event materials — without losing legibility or warmth.
vii.
Solid wordmark · technical version
The flat-color wordmark covered in section 04, for any application where the granulated texture cannot be preserved (small embroidery, single-color reproduction).

  • Reconsider · explore an alternative direction
iii.

Confirm color foundations

Section 07. Approve the working palette (trans pride colors + peach/lavender extension) and the Fraunces + Inter typography pairing as the system foundations.

  • Approve with adjustments (specify which)
  • Request alternatives

Open feedback — anything else?

Beyond the three decisions above, any reactions, concerns, or instincts are welcome. What feels right? What doesn't sit with you? Is there a sentiment, story, or constituency this proposal isn't holding well enough? This is still your brand — your read on it matters more than mine.

10
What happens next

After your approval.

Once the three decisions above are confirmed, a full brand guide will be developed — locking the system in place for every application MSTN will need.

The full brand guide will cover: final logo files in all formats and variations (SVG, PNG, PDF for print, EPS for vector); full color system with hex, RGB, CMYK, and Pantone equivalents; complete typography hierarchy with weights, sizes, and use cases; logo clear space, minimum size, and don'ts; social media templates; email signature templates; merch application guidelines; and document templates for grant writing and donor communications.

This document is a proposal, not a finish line. It exists so the system that follows is built on shared agreement — not assumption.
