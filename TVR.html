School & Group Performance Poster · Season 26–27 · May 2026
I.

The Final Poster

A booking poster directed at educators — built to look like a beloved storybook page.

This poster is designed for a specific audience: English-speaking teachers who book Cazateatro performances for their schools. It is not a public event poster — there is no date, no venue, no ticket price. Instead, the poster functions as a single-page sales tool: it communicates the title, the bilingual nature of the production, the literary source, and a path to booking.

Every visual decision flows from that purpose. The aesthetic borrows directly from the world of the original 1922 book — aged paper, hand-drawn watercolor, classical typography — so that when a teacher sees this on a desk, in an inbox, or pinned to a staff-room corkboard, the response is immediate: this is a real story I already love, performed by people who respect it.

II.

The Illustration

Honoring Mónica's direction to use the original rabbit from Margery Williams' book.

Mónica's specific request shaped the entire visual identity of this piece: use the original Velveteen Rabbit — the watercolor illustration that has accompanied the story since its first publication in 1922. This was not a stylistic preference. It was a strategic one.

Teachers don't need to be sold on The Velveteen Rabbit. They already love it. They taught it last year. Their own teachers taught it to them. The job of this poster is not to introduce the story — it is to remind them of it.

Using the original illustration accomplishes three things at once. First, it triggers immediate recognition — the rabbit is part of educators' shared visual vocabulary. Second, it signals respect for the source material, which matters to teachers evaluating whether a theatre company will handle a beloved book with care. Third, it positions Cazateatro's bilingual adaptation as a continuation of the book's tradition rather than a departure from it.

The hand-drawn watercolor quality — the soft sepia tones, the visible brushstrokes, the slightly worn texture — also does aesthetic work that no modern illustration could. It communicates handmade, intimate, treasured. Those are the qualities of a children's theatre piece worth booking.

III.

Key Design Decisions

Why each element looks the way it does.

Decision 01

English first, Spanish in parentheses

Cazateatro's brand standard is Spanish first, English second — but that rule serves a community audience. This poster is a booking tool aimed at English-speaking school administrators and teachers. Leading in English gets the title recognized instantly, while (El Conejo de Terciopelo) below signals the bilingual production format. The hierarchy serves the audience, not the rule.

Decision 02

No date, no venue, no price

School performances are booked, not sold to walk-up audiences. Dates are negotiated per booking; venues are the schools themselves. Adding placeholder details would clutter the poster and confuse its function. Instead, a single QR code routes every interested teacher straight to the booking form.

Decision 03

Aged paper, not pure white

The cream/sepia background frames the rabbit illustration in its native habitat — the page of an old storybook. It also gives the poster warmth that pure white would strip away. The subtle paper texture reinforces the handmade quality and prevents the design from feeling like a flyer printed at Staples.

Decision 04

Red reserved for the title only

Earlier drafts used Cazateatro red across the title, body copy, contact information, and the decorative frame — over-saturating the poster and forcing the title to compete with everything else for attention. Pulling the red back to the title alone — with all supporting text in sepia brown — gives the title its impact back and lets the rabbit be the emotional center.

Decision 05

Sepia brown for all supporting text

The body copy, QR instructions, contact information, and decorative frame all sit in a deep sepia brown that lives in the same tonal family as the watercolor rabbit. The text reads like ink notations on aged paper rather than overlaid graphic elements. The result is a unified visual world rather than a poster with text stuck on top of an image.

Decision 06

Black QR code, not red

A small but important functional choice. Decorative QR colors fail to scan more often than designers expect — especially in low light, on textured paper, or with older phone cameras. A booking opportunity lost to a stylized QR is a booking lost. Black on cream is the most reliable combination and reads as deliberate, not as a compromise.

IV.

Typography System

Two families. Clear roles. Continuity with the rest of the season.

The typography uses two complementary families, each with a specific job. Cormorant Garamond is a contemporary revival of classical Garamond — the serif lineage that has defined storybook covers and literary editions for centuries. Raleway is a clean humanist sans-serif that handles functional information without competing with the title for attention.

Season continuity. Cormorant Garamond is the same serif family used for the title of Our Lady of la Tortilla and for the body copy in The Last Christmas. This is intentional. When a teacher receives this poster and later sees other Cazateatro materials throughout the season, the typography signals shared authorship before any logo or name is processed. It is the visual handwriting of the company.

Pairing it with Raleway — rather than another serif — creates a clear hierarchy between the literary register of the title (Cormorant) and the practical register of the booking information (Raleway). Two voices, doing two jobs, in one cohesive document.

V.

Color Palette

A restrained palette drawn from the world of the book itself.

The palette is built around the natural tonal range of the original watercolor illustration. Rather than imposing Cazateatro's standard brand colors onto the design, the brand red is used sparingly — as an accent that ties the piece back to the company while letting the storybook world breathe.

The principle: one bold accent, surrounded by quiet tones. When every element shouts, nothing is heard. By restraining the red to the title, that single moment of brand color does more work than red splashed across every element ever could.

VI.

Within the Season

How this piece sits inside the larger 26–27 visual language.

Each production in the 26–27 season has its own aesthetic world — and that is the point. Bilingual children's theatre, original holiday drama, and contemporary satire ask for different visual registers. But underneath those distinct worlds, a shared typographic and color sensibility ties them together as one company's body of work.

  • The Velveteen Rabbit / El Conejo de Terciopelo School Booking · Storybook Vintage
  • The Last Christmas December 2026 · Dos Mundos Minimalist
  • Christmas Fiesta December 2026 · Pesebre Clásico
  • Our Lady of la Tortilla April 2027 · Watercolor Editorial

The thread between them is Cormorant Garamond. Each title appears in this serif family — with different weights, treatments, and supporting families — so that any audience member who follows Cazateatro through the season experiences a single, evolving typographic voice rather than four unrelated visual identities.

The Velveteen Rabbit's contribution to that voice is its storybook register: warmer, softer, more handmade. The piece exists at the children's-literature end of Cazateatro's range and looks the part — while still speaking the same typographic language as everything else the company will produce this year.
